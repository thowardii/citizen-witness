## 2. Speech Analysis

**What was the speech?**

Pulliam was filming law enforcement officers performing their duties in public — a welfare check on a mentally ill person, conducted on a public street in Damon, Texas. He stood across the street, stationary, not interfering. He was acting as an independent journalist / First Amendment auditor, gathering footage for his YouTube channel ("Justin Pulliam Show").

**Was it protected?**

Yes. The right to film police performing their duties in public is clearly established First Amendment activity. The key precedents:

- *Glik v. Cunniffe* (1st Cir., 2011): "The filming of government officials engaged in their duties in a public place... fits comfortably within the basic principles of First Amendment protection"
- *Turner v. Lieutenant Driver* (5th Cir., 2017): "The First Amendment protects the right to film police, subject only to reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions"
- *Fields v. City of Philadelphia* (3rd Cir., 2017): "The First Amendment protects the act of photographing, filming, or otherwise recording police officers conducting their official duties in public"

**The body camera proves the protection:** The deputies' own body-worn cameras recorded Pulliam standing still, at a distance, not interfering. The activity was not just protected in theory — the government's own evidence confirmed he was exercising the right peacefully.

## 3. Probable Cause Check — The Nieves/Gonzalez Gateway

**Stated charge:** Interference with public duties (Texas Penal Code § 38.15)

**The government's theory:** Pulliam was "interfering" with the deputies' execution of the welfare check by filming them.

**Pulliam's position:** He was standing across the street, stationary, not speaking to the deputies or the subject of the welfare check. Filming is not interference.

**Did the officer state probable cause?**

Rollins claimed Pulliam was interfering. But the body camera footage — the government's own evidence — showed Pulliam standing still, at a distance, making no physical contact and issuing no verbal commands or disruptions. The federal court's ruling that the arrest violated the First Amendment is effectively a finding that the probable cause was pretextual.

### 🔍 DETECTION HOOK 3 — Charge Mismatch

| Element | Assessment |
|---------|------------|
| Stated charge | Interference with public duties (Texas § 38.15) |
| Actual conduct | Standing across the street, filming, not interacting with deputies or subject |
| Body cam evidence | Confirms no interference — Pulliam stationary and silent |
| Charge classification | **PRETEXTUAL** — charge does not match conduct. The "interference" was the act of filming itself |
| Nieves/Gonzalez gateway | Pulliam CAN clear the probable-cause bar because objective evidence (body cam) proves the charge was pretextual. Under Gonzalez, he does not need a comparator — he has direct video evidence |

**Assessment:** This is a clean charge-mismatch case. The statute requires actual interference — physical obstruction, verbal disruption, or active hindrance. None of those are present on the body cam. The charge was the interference itself: the act of filming. This is the category error the NotebookLM synthesis identified, applied to a filming-police context instead of a parody or petition context.

### 🔍 DETECTION HOOK 4 — Officer Statements

The court record establishes that Rollins's motivation was "hostility" toward Pulliam's filming. Specific officer statements from available records:

- Rollins ordered Pulliam to stop recording → this is the officer's own statement about the protected activity. He identified the filming as the problem, not any actual interference. The order itself is evidence of retaliatory motive.
- The IJ press release characterizes the arrest as "motivated by Rollins's hostility" — suggesting Rollins made additional statements (verbal or in the arrest report) expressing hostility toward the filming, which became part of the court record.

**What we need for a complete analysis:** The exact verbatim statements from Rollins at the scene. The body camera audio would capture these. "Stop filming" / "Turn off the camera" / "You're interfering" — the exact words matter for distinguishing between an officer who believed (wrongly) that filming is interference vs. an officer who knew the filming was protected and arrested anyway out of hostility.

## 5. Pattern Match — The New Lane: Filming-Police Retaliation

| Element | Bailey | Novak | Gonzalez | **Pulliam** |
|---------|--------|-------|----------|-------------|
| Speech type | Facebook joke (zombie) | Facebook parody (police satire) | Petition (remove city manager) | **Filming police (auditor)** |
| Speech ambiguity | High | Medium | Zero | **Zero** |

| Charge | Terrorism | Disrupting public services (felony) | Tampering with government records | **Interference with public duties** |

| Body cam exists? | Unknown | No (2016) | Yes (surveillance video) | **Yes — and it proved non-interference** |
| Device seized? | No | Yes (phone + laptop) | No (document dispute) | **Yes (camera/phone)** |
| Prosecution | DA refused | Prosecuted, acquitted | Charges dropped | **Charges dropped or resolved** |
| Civil outcome | $205K damages | QI blocked remedy | SCOTUS revived claim | **$75K compensatory damages** |
| Key legal issue | Satire → threat classification | Parody → QI | Petition → Nieves exception | **Filming → pretextual interference charge** |

**Identical spine, new lane:** All four cases share the same structure — protected speech → officer takes personal offense → arrest on a charge that is technically available but pretextual → criminal case collapses → §1983 retaliation suit → damages or doctrine.

**What Pulliam adds:**

Pulliam opens a fourth lane: the First Amendment auditor / cop-watcher pattern. This is distinct from Bailey (satire-as-threat), Novak (parody-as-disruption), and Gonzalez (petition-as-tampering). The pattern is: filming-as-interference. The officer sees the camera as a challenge to authority, reclassifies the protected activity as obstruction, and arrests.

**The body cam paradox:** In Pulliam, the body camera was the evidence that vindicated him — it proved he was standing still. In a case where the body camera was off, malfunctioning, or the footage was "lost," this would be a fundamentally different case. The existence of body cam footage here is both the proof of the violation AND the flag for what's missing in other cases.
